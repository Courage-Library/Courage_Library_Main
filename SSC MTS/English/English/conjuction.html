Conjunctions (English Grammar)

SSC MTS & Delhi Police Exams - English Language

1. What is a Conjunction?

A Conjunction is a word that joins words, phrases, or clauses together in a sentence. It acts like a bridge that connects ideas and makes a sentence smooth and meaningful.

Simple Definition: A conjunction is a word used to connect two or more words, phrases, or sentences.

Ram and Shyam are friends.

She is poor but honest.

I will call you when I reach home.

2. Types of Conjunctions

Type Function Examples
Coordinating Conjunctions Join two equal parts (words, phrases, or independent clauses) and, but, or, nor, for, yet, so
Subordinating Conjunctions Join a dependent (subordinate) clause to a main clause because, although, since, when, if, unless, before, after, till, while
Correlative Conjunctions Used in pairs to join balanced sentence parts either...or, neither...nor, both...and, not only...but also, whether...or

3. Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS Rule)

The seven coordinating conjunctions are:
For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So

Conjunction Use Example
For Reason He stayed home, for he was sick.
And Addition She is kind and helpful.
Nor Negative addition He doesn't eat meat, nor does he drink milk.
But Contrast She is rich but unhappy.
Or Choice Tea or coffee?
Yet Contrast (similar to but) It was raining, yet we went out.
So Result He was tired, so he slept early.

4. Subordinating Conjunctions

Subordinating conjunctions join dependent clauses with main clauses.

Function Conjunctions Example
Cause / Reason because, since, as He failed because he was lazy.
Condition if, unless, provided that If it rains, we'll stay home.
Time when, before, after, till, while Wait here till I come.
Contrast although, though, even though Although he is old, he is active.
Purpose so that, in order that Study hard so that you can pass.
Result that He was so tired that he slept instantly.

5. Correlative Conjunctions

Correlative conjunctions always appear in pairs and balance two equal sentence parts.

Conjunction Pair Example
Either...or You can either stay or leave.
Neither...nor He is neither rich nor poor.
Both...and Both Riya and Neha are teachers.
Not only...but also He is not only smart but also hardworking.
Whether...or I don't know whether he'll come or not.

6. Common Rules and Tips

‚úÖ Rule 1: Comma Usage

Use a comma before a coordinating conjunction joining two independent clauses.

Example: I wanted to go out, but it was raining.

‚úÖ Rule 2: Avoid Double Conjunctions

Don't use two conjunctions together unnecessarily.

‚ùå Although but he was late.
‚úÖ Although he was late.

‚úÖ Rule 3: Parallel Structure

In correlative conjunctions, use the same grammatical structure in both parts.

Example: He is not only rich but also generous.

‚úÖ Rule 4: Dependent Clauses

Subordinating conjunctions start dependent clauses, which cannot stand alone.

‚ùå Because I was tired
‚úÖ I slept early because I was tired.

7. Common Delhi Police / SSC Exam Facts

‚úÖ

"FANBOYS" are the most common coordinating conjunctions.

‚úÖ

"Because," "although," "if," and "since" are frequent in error detection.

‚úÖ

‚úÖ

8. Quick Recap

Type Function Examples
Coordinating Joins equal parts and, but, or, so
Subordinating Joins dependent clause because, when, although
Correlative Joins in pairs either...or, neither...nor
