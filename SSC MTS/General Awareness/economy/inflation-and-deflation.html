Inflation & Deflation

Economics - Price Level Concepts - Complete Notes for SSC MTS, GD, CHSL

What is Inflation?

Inflation is a general increase in prices of goods and services over time, leading to a decrease in the purchasing power of money.

Simple Example

Milk price: ₹50 → ₹60 → ₹70 (inflation)

With same ₹100, you can buy less milk than before.

Key Characteristics

Price Increase: General rise across economy
Purchasing Power: Money buys less than before
Persistent: Not temporary price fluctuation
Widespread: Affects most goods/services

Memory Trick

"More price, less purchasing power."

Prices increase → purchasing power of money decreases.

What is Deflation?

Deflation is a general decrease in prices of goods and services over time, usually caused by reduced demand in the economy.

Simple Example

Mobile price: ₹20,000 → ₹18,000 → ₹16,000 (deflation)

With same ₹20,000, you can buy better mobile or save money.

Key Characteristics

Price Decrease: General fall across economy
Low Demand: Usually caused by economic slowdown
Purchasing Power: Money buys more than before
Economic Impact: Can lead to recession if prolonged

Memory Trick

"Demand down → Prices down."

Reduced consumer demand leads to falling prices.

Inflation vs Deflation (SSC's Favorite)

Concept Meaning Effect on Prices Effect on Economy
InflationGeneral rise in pricesPrices increaseMoney loses value
DeflationGeneral fall in pricesPrices decreaseEconomy slows down

Types of Inflation (VERY IMPORTANT)

1. Demand-Pull Inflation

Demand increases but supply cannot match → Prices rise.

Example: Diwali season price rise

Trick: "Too much money chasing too few goods."

2. Cost-Push Inflation

Production cost increases → Producers raise prices.

Costlier raw materials
Higher wages
Higher oil prices

Trick: "Costs push the prices up."

3. Built-in Inflation

Workers demand higher wages → companies raise prices → cycle continues.

Also called: Wage-Price Spiral

Other Important Inflation Types (SSC MUST)

Creeping Inflation

Prices rise slowly (1–3% yearly). Considered healthy for growth.

Walking Inflation

Moderate rise (3–10% yearly). Requires policy attention.

Running Inflation

Rapid rise (10–20% yearly). Dangerous for economy.

Hyperinflation

Extremely high (over 50% monthly). Economic collapse.

Examples: Zimbabwe, Venezuela

Stagflation

Inflation + Economic stagnation + High unemployment.

Trick: "Stag = Stuck + Inflation."

Disinflation

Reduction in inflation rate, not negative prices.

Example: 7% → 4% → 2% inflation rate

Measurement of Inflation (VERY IMPORTANT)

WPI (Wholesale Price Index)
Measures: Price changes at wholesale level
Published by: Office of Economic Adviser (Ministry of Commerce)
Base Year: 2011–12
Covers: 697 commodities
CPI (Consumer Price Index)
Measures: Price changes at retail/consumer level
Published by: NSO (National Statistical Office)
Base Year: 2012
Used by RBI: For inflation targeting

CPI Categories

CPI-R (Rural)
CPI-U (Urban)
CPI-Combined
CPI-AL (Agri Labour)
CPI-RL (Rural Labour)

India's Inflation Target

4% ± 2%

i.e., between 2% to 6% (RBI uses CPI-Combined)

Memory Trick for WPI vs CPI

"CPI = Consumers, WPI = Wholesalers."

CPI

Consumer/Retail level

WPI

Wholesale/Bulk level

Causes & Effects of Inflation

Causes of Inflation

Demand-side Causes
Higher income levels
Increased government spending
Excessive money supply
High export demand
Supply-side Causes
Costlier raw materials
Rising fuel prices
Natural disasters
Supply chain disruptions

Effects of Inflation

Positive Effects
Encourages production
Good for borrowers (repay with cheaper money)
Reduces real value of debt
Negative Effects
Reduces purchasing power
Hurts fixed-income groups
Increases income inequality
Reduces savings rate

Control of Inflation

Monetary Policy (by RBI)
Increase Repo Rate: Makes borrowing costlier
Increase CRR: Banks keep more reserves with RBI
Increase SLR: Banks maintain more liquid assets
Open Market Operations: Sell government bonds
Fiscal Policy (by Government)
Reduce Government Spending: Cuts unnecessary expenditure
Increase Taxes: Reduces disposable income
Control Public Debt: Limits borrowing
Supply-side Measures: Increase production

Memory Trick for Inflation Control

"Raise rates → Reduce inflation."

RBI raises interest rates to make borrowing expensive, reducing money supply and inflation.

SSC MTS Practice Questions

1. Inflation refers to?

General rise in prices of goods and services

2. Deflation refers to?

General fall in prices of goods and services

3. Demand-pull inflation happens when?

Demand exceeds supply in the economy

4. Cost-push inflation happens when?

Production costs increase, forcing producers to raise prices

5. WPI measures prices at which level?

Wholesale level (bulk transactions)

6. CPI is used for?

Measuring retail prices / RBI's inflation targeting

7. What is the base year of WPI in India?

2011–12

8. What is the base year of CPI in India?

2012

9. Stagflation includes which conditions?

High inflation + Economic stagnation + High unemployment

10. What is India's inflation target range?

4% ± 2% (i.e., between 2% to 6%)

Conclusion

Understanding inflation and deflation is crucial for economics questions in SSC exams. These concepts explain how price levels affect the economy and are tested through definitions, types, measurement, and control measures.

Key Takeaways

Inflation: General price rise, reduces purchasing power
Deflation: General price fall, indicates economic slowdown
Types: Demand-pull, Cost-push, Built-in, Stagflation
Measurement: WPI (wholesale), CPI (retail), CPI used by RBI
Base Years: WPI (2011–12), CPI (2012)
Inflation Target: 4% ± 2% for India
Control: Monetary policy (RBI rates), Fiscal policy (govt spending/taxes)
Memory Tricks: "More price, less purchasing power", "CPI = Consumers, WPI = Wholesalers", "Raise rates → Reduce inflation"

This topic absolutely guarantees 2–3 marks in SSC MTS, GD, and CHSL exams.
