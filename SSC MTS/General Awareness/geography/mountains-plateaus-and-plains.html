Mountains, Plateaus & Plains

Complete Notes for SSC MTS, GD, CHSL - Geography

Introduction

Earth's surface features three major landforms: mountains, plateaus, and plains. Understanding their formation, characteristics, and examples is essential for geography questions in SSC exams.

SSC MTS frequently asks about types of mountains, plateau locations, plains of India, and how they are formed.

1. Mountains

What are Mountains?

Mountains are high elevated landforms with steep slopes, usually rising more than 600 meters above surrounding land.

They are important as climate barriers, sources of rivers, and centers of biodiversity.

Mountains cover about 27% of Earth's land surface and are home to 15% of world's population.

Types of Mountains (VERY IMPORTANT)

A. Fold Mountains

Formed when tectonic plates collide and fold the Earth's crust.

Examples (World):

Himalayas (Asia)
Alps (Europe)
Andes (S. America)
Rockies (N. America)

Exam Trick

"H–A–A–R"

Himalaya, Alps, Andes, Rockies

These are young & high mountains, still growing.

B. Block Mountains

Formed due to faulting – land rises or sinks along cracks in Earth's crust.

Examples:

Vindhya Range (India)
Satpura Range (India)
Harz Mountains (Germany)
Sierra Nevada (USA)

Also called Horst & Graben.

Horst = raised block, Graben = sunken block.

C. Volcanic Mountains

Formed by lava deposition from volcanic eruptions.

Examples:

Mount Kilimanjaro (Africa)
Mount Fujiyama (Japan)
Mount Vesuvius (Italy)
Mount St. Helens (USA)

Often conical in shape with a crater at the top.

Can be active, dormant, or extinct volcanoes.

2. Plateaus

What are Plateaus?

Plateaus are flat-topped elevated regions with steep sides, resembling a table.

Also called tablelands. They cover about 45% of Earth's land surface.

Characteristics: High altitude, relatively flat top, steep sides, rich in minerals.

Types of Plateaus

A. Intermontane Plateaus

Located between mountains.

Tibetan Plateau

Largest plateau in the world

B. Piedmont Plateaus

Located at the foot of mountains.

Patagonia Plateau

South America

C. Continental Plateaus

Raised from the sea floor, surrounded by plains or oceans.

Deccan Plateau

India (largest in India)

Major Plateaus of India

Deccan Plateau

Largest in India

Chota Nagpur Plateau

Mineral-rich region

Malwa Plateau

Central India

Rajasthan Plateau

Aravalli Range area

Importance of Plateaus

  • Mineral-rich areas – source of coal, iron, manganese
  • Good for mining activities
  • Origin of many rivers
  • Support agriculture in some regions
  • Important for hydroelectric power generation
  • Rich in biodiversity and forest resources

3. Plains

What are Plains?

Plains are flat lowlands formed by river deposits or sea deposits.

They are the most densely populated and agriculturally productive regions on Earth.

Characteristics: Flat, fertile, low elevation, suitable for agriculture and human settlement.

Types of Plains

A. Structural Plains

Formed by uplift or submergence of land areas.

Example:

Great Plains of USA

Formed by uplift of continental shelf

Relatively flat regions formed by geological processes rather than river deposition.

B. Depositional Plains (MOST IMPORTANT)

Formed by river deposits / alluvium over thousands of years.

Major Examples:

Indo-Gangetic Plains (India)

Formed by Ganga, Brahmaputra, Indus rivers

Mississippi River Plains (USA)

Formed by Mississippi River deposits

Nile River Plains (Egypt)

Formed by Nile River deposits

These are the world's most fertile agricultural regions and support high population density.

C. Erosional Plains

Formed by erosion over long periods, wearing down mountains.

Example:

Peneplains

Old mountains eroded to almost flat plains

These plains represent the final stage in the erosion cycle of landforms.

Indian Plains (VERY IMPORTANT FOR SSC)

Indo-Gangetic Plain

  • States covered: Punjab, Haryana, UP, Bihar, West Bengal
  • Formed by Ganga, Brahmaputra, and Indus rivers
  • Fertile alluvium soil suitable for agriculture
  • Most densely populated region in India

Features of Indo-Gangetic Plain:

Fertile soil
Dense population
Major agricultural zone
River network

Memory Trick & Summary

Mountains

"FBV"

Fold, Block, Volcanic

High elevated, steep slopes

Plateaus

"Tablelands"

High & Flat top

Rich in minerals

Plains

Low & Flat, fertile

Quick Revision Table

Landform How Formed Examples Key Feature
Fold Mountains Plate collision & folding Himalayas, Andes, Alps High & young mountains
Block Mountains Faulting of crust Vindhya, Satpura, Harz Steep block-like structure
Volcanic Mountains Lava deposition Kilimanjaro, Fujiyama Volcanic cones with craters
Plateaus Uplift/lava flows Deccan, Tibetan, Chota Nagpur Flat top, steep sides
Plains River deposits/alluvium Indo-Gangetic, Mississippi Flat, fertile, densely populated

SSC MTS Practice Questions

1. The Himalayas belong to which category of mountains?

Fold Mountains (formed by tectonic plate collision)

2. Which is the largest plateau in the world?

Tibetan Plateau (Intermontane plateau in Asia)

3. Block mountains are formed due to?

Faulting (cracking and displacement of Earth's crust)

4. Which is the most fertile plain of India?

Indo-Gangetic Plain (formed by river deposits)

5. The Deccan Plateau was formed from?

Volcanic lava flows (basalt rocks)

6. Mount Kilimanjaro is a?

Volcanic Mountain (dormant volcano in Africa)

7. Vindhya and Satpura ranges are examples of?

Block Mountains (formed by faulting in India)

8. What type of plain is formed by river deposits?

Depositional Plain or Alluvial Plain

Conclusion

Understanding Earth's major landforms – mountains, plateaus, and plains – is fundamental to physical geography. Each has distinct formation processes, characteristics, and significance for human life and economic activities.

Final Summary

Mountains → High elevation (Fold/Block/Volcanic)
Plateaus → High + Flat top (Mineral-rich)
Plains → Low + Flat (Fertile, densely populated)
Formation: Fold/Fault/Lava for mountains; Uplift for plateaus; River deposits for plains
