Climate of India

Complete Notes for SSC MTS, GD, CHSL - Geography

Introduction

India has a Tropical Monsoon Climate characterized by distinct seasons and seasonal reversal of winds. Understanding India's climate patterns, monsoon mechanism, and local winds is essential for SSC geography preparation.

SSC frequently asks about monsoon seasons, local winds, rainfall patterns, and factors affecting India's climate.

India's Tropical Monsoon Climate

Key Characteristics

India's climate is dominated by the monsoon system with clear seasonal patterns.

Seasonal reversal of winds
Distinct wet and dry seasons
Regional variations in temperature and rainfall
Himalayas act as climate barrier

Why Monsoon Climate?

  • Large landmass with differential heating
  • Himalayas block cold Central Asian winds
  • Seasonal shift of pressure belts
  • Proximity to Indian Ocean

Factors Affecting India's Climate

Primary Factors

1. Latitude

Tropic of Cancer divides India into tropical (south) and subtropical (north) regions

2. Altitude

Higher altitude = Cooler temperature (Shimla cooler than Delhi)

3. Distance from Sea

Coastal areas have moderate climate; interiors have extreme temperatures

Secondary Factors

4. Himalayan Mountains

Block cold winds from Central Asia, trap monsoon winds

5. Monsoon Winds

Seasonal wind reversal brings 75% of India's rainfall

6. Ocean Currents

Affect coastal climate patterns

Memory Trick

"L A D H M"

Latitude – Altitude – Distance – Himalayas – Monsoon

Five key factors affecting India's climate

Seasons of India (Four Major Seasons)

1. Winter Season (December–February)

North India: Cold temperatures, frost in some areas
South India: Mild and pleasant weather
Winds: Dry northeast trade winds
Clear skies, low humidity

Local Phenomena:

Western Disturbances
Mango Showers
Key Features

Western Disturbances: Bring winter rain to North India (important for rabi crops)

Mango Showers: Pre-monsoon showers in Kerala & Karnataka help mango ripening

2. Summer Season (March–May)

High temperatures across India
Low pressure develops in northwest India
Pre-monsoon thunderstorms
Heat waves common in northern plains
Local Hot Winds
Loo: Hot, dry winds in North India plains
Kalbaisakhi: Thunderstorms in West Bengal
Norwesters: Evening thunderstorms in Assam & Bengal

3. Southwest Monsoon (June–September)

Brings 75% of India's annual rainfall. Arrives in Kerala around June 1st.

Two Branches:

Arabian Sea Branch

Hits Western Ghats first, causes heavy rainfall on windward side

Bay of Bengal Branch

Moves towards NE India, causes heavy rainfall in Cherapunji/Mawsynram

Important Facts
Mawsynram: Highest rainfall in India & world
Monsoon Break: Dry spells during monsoon season
Rain Shadow: Deccan plateau east of Western Ghats gets less rain
Advancing Monsoon: June-July (peak rainfall)

4. Retreating Monsoon (October–November)

Monsoon winds retreat southwards
Clear skies, rise in temperature (October heat)
High humidity, uncomfortable weather
Cyclonic activities in Bay of Bengal
Key Features

Tamil Nadu Rainfall: Receives bulk of rainfall from retreating monsoon

Cyclones: Bay of Bengal experiences tropical cyclones

Harvest Season: Major harvesting period for kharif crops

Special Local Winds of India

Wind Region Season Description
Loo North India Plains Summer Hot, dry winds causing heat waves
Kalbaisakhi West Bengal April-May Thunderstorms with rain, hail
Norwesters Assam & Bengal Pre-monsoon Evening thunderstorms, also called "Kalbaisakhi"
Mango Showers Kerala & Karnataka Pre-monsoon Light showers helping mango ripening
Western Disturbances North India Winter Bring winter rain, important for rabi crops

Monsoon Mechanism (Super Important)

Why Monsoon Comes to India?

1. Differential Heating

Land heats faster than ocean in summer → Low pressure over Indian subcontinent

2. Shift of ITCZ

Inter Tropical Convergence Zone moves northward in summer, attracting monsoon winds

3. Pressure Systems

High pressure over Indian Ocean, low pressure over land creates pressure gradient

El Niño & La Niña Effects

El Niño

Warming of Pacific Ocean → Weak monsoon in India

Associated with drought years in India

La Niña

Cooling of Pacific Ocean → Strong monsoon in India

Associated with good rainfall years

Rainfall Pattern in India

High Rainfall Areas (>200 cm)

Western Ghats windward side
North-East India (Meghalaya, Assam)
West Bengal, Coastal areas
Himalayan foothills

Mawsynram, Meghalaya

Highest rainfall in India & world (~11,872 mm annually)

Low Rainfall Areas (<50 cm)

Western Rajasthan (Thar Desert)
Ladakh region
Deccan Plateau interior
Rain shadow areas of Western Ghats

Jaisalmer, Rajasthan

Lowest rainfall in India (~12 cm annually)

SSC MTS Practice Questions

1. What type of climate does India have?

Tropical Monsoon Climate

2. Which place receives the highest rainfall in India?

Mawsynram, Meghalaya

3. During which season does the "Loo" blow in North India?

Summer season (March-May)

4. Which Indian state receives monsoon rainfall first?

Kerala (around June 1st)

5. Tamil Nadu receives most rainfall from which monsoon?

Retreating monsoon (Northeast monsoon)

6. What brings winter rainfall to North India?

Western Disturbances

7. Which season receives maximum rainfall in India?

Southwest Monsoon (June-September)

8. What are the two branches of Southwest monsoon?

Arabian Sea Branch and Bay of Bengal Branch

Seasons

"Loo = Summer"

Western Disturbances = Winter

Kalbaisakhi = Pre-monsoon

Conclusion

Understanding the climate of India is crucial for grasping the country's agricultural patterns, water resources, and regional variations. The monsoon system, with its seasonal rhythms and local phenomena, shapes life across the subcontinent.

Final Summary

Highest Rainfall → Mawsynram, Meghalaya
Lowest Rainfall → Jaisalmer, Rajasthan
Summer Winds → Loo (hot & dry)
Winter Rainfall → Western Disturbances
Monsoon Branches → Arabian Sea & Bay of Bengal
