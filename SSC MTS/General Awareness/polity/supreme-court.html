Supreme Court of India

Indian Polity - Judiciary - Complete Notes for SSC MTS, GD, CHSL

Introduction (Articles 124â€“147, Part V)

The Supreme Court of India is the highest court and the final interpreter of the Constitution. It serves as the guardian of the Constitution and protector of Fundamental Rights.

Highest judicial authority

Protects constitutional values

Protector of FRs

Composition of Supreme Court

Structure & Appointment

34 Judges

1 Chief Justice of India + 33 other judges

Appointment Process

Appointed by President on advice of Collegium System (CJI + 4 senior-most SC judges).

Tenure & Retirement

Supreme Court judges retire at 65 years of age. Can resign anytime.

Qualifications

Citizenship: Must be a citizen of India
Judicial Experience: Judge of High Court for 5 years
Advocacy Experience: Advocate in High Court for 10 years
Distinguished Jurist: Recognized as an eminent jurist
Removal of Judges (Article 124(4))

Supreme Court judges can be removed ONLY through impeachment by Parliament on grounds of:

Proven misbehavior
Incapacity

Requires special majority in both Houses of Parliament.

Jurisdiction of Supreme Court (VERY IMPORTANT)

1. Original Jurisdiction (Article 131)

SC directly hears disputes between:

Centre vs State
State vs State
Example: Interstate water disputes
2. Appellate Jurisdiction

SC hears appeals from High Courts on:

Civil cases
Criminal cases
Constitutional cases
Special Leave Petition (SLP - Article 136)
3. Advisory Jurisdiction (Article 143)

President can seek SC's opinion on constitutional matters.

SC's advice is not binding on President.

4. Writ Jurisdiction (Article 32)

Protector of Fundamental Rights. Issues 5 writs:

Habeas Corpus
Mandamus
Certiorari
Prohibition
Quo-Warranto
5. Judicial Review

SC can cancel any law that violates:

Constitution
Fundamental Rights

Part of Basic Structure Doctrine

6. Public Interest Litigation (PIL)

Allows any citizen to approach court for public issues.

Environmental issues
Human rights violations
Corruption matters

Introduced by Justice P. N. Bhagwati

Important Articles Related to Supreme Court

Article 124: Establishment of SC, appointment of judges
Article 125: Salaries of judges
Article 131: Original jurisdiction
Article 132-134: Appellate jurisdiction
Article 136: Special Leave Petition (SLP)
Article 137: Review of judgments
Article 141: SC judgments binding on all courts
Article 143: Advisory jurisdiction
Article 145: SC rules and procedure
Article 146: Officers and staff of SC

Important Supreme Court Cases

Kesavananda Bharati Case (1973)

Established the Basic Structure Doctrine. Parliament cannot amend the basic structure of Constitution.

Maneka Gandhi Case (1978)

Expanded Article 21 (Right to Life) to include right to live with human dignity.

Golaknath Case (1967)

Initially held that Fundamental Rights cannot be amended (later overruled by Kesavananda Bharati).

Minerva Mills Case (1980)

Reaffirmed Basic Structure Doctrine and established balance between Fundamental Rights and DPSPs.

Shah Bano Case (1985)

Upheld maintenance rights for Muslim women, leading to political controversy.

S. R. Bommai Case (1994)

Limited arbitrary imposition of President's Rule, strengthened federalism.

SSC MTS Practice Questions

1. When was the Supreme Court of India established?

1950

2. Which Article deals with Original Jurisdiction of Supreme Court?

Article 131

3. Under which Article does President seek advice from Supreme Court?

Article 143

4. Who appoints judges of the Supreme Court?

President of India

5. What is the retirement age of a Supreme Court judge?

65 years

6. Which Article gives Supreme Court power to issue writs?

Article 32

7. Which case established the Basic Structure Doctrine?

Kesavananda Bharati Case (1973)

8. CJI plus 4 senior-most judges form which system?

Collegium System

9. Which Article deals with Special Leave Petition (SLP)?

Article 136

10. What makes Supreme Court the protector of Fundamental Rights?

Writ Jurisdiction under Article 32

Conclusion

The Supreme Court of India is a cornerstone of Indian democracy, ensuring constitutional supremacy and protecting citizens' rights. Understanding its structure, powers, and landmark judgments is crucial for SSC exams.

Key Takeaways

Composition: 34 judges (1 CJI + 33 others), appointed by President via Collegium
Retirement: 65 years, removal only by impeachment
Jurisdictions: Original, Appellate, Advisory, Writ, Judicial Review
Important Articles: 124, 131, 136, 143, 32
Landmark Cases: Kesavananda Bharati (Basic Structure), Maneka Gandhi (Article 21)
PIL: Public Interest Litigation for social issues
Memory Tricks: "O-A-A" for jurisdictions, "G-P-D-J" for functions, "65-62" for retirement ages
