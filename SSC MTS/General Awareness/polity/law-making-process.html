The Law-Making Process in India

How a Bill Becomes a Law - Complete Notes for SSC MTS, GD, CHSL

Types of Bills in India

Ordinary Bill

Concerns general matters like education, health, crime, etc.

Can be introduced in either House

Money Bill (Article 110)

Deals with taxes, Consolidated Fund, government expenditure.

Only in Lok Sabha, Speaker certifies

Finance Bill

Contains financial proposals and other guidelines.

Two types: Type I (Money Bill), Type II (Ordinary)

Constitutional Amendment Bill (Article 368)

To amend the Constitution, requires special majority.

No Joint Sitting allowed

How an Ordinary Bill Becomes a Law

1

Introduction (First Reading)

A minister or MP introduces the Bill in either House. The Bill is published in the Gazette.

Note: This is the formal introduction stage without debate.

2

Second Reading (Most Important Stage)

General Discussion

Members discuss the purpose and principles of the Bill.

Committee Stage

Bill sent to Departmental Standing Committee or Select Committee for detailed examination.

Clause-by-Clause

Detailed discussion, amendments proposed and voted upon.

3

Third Reading

Final version of the Bill is put to vote. If passed, it goes to the other House for consideration.

4

Passing in the Other House

The second House can approve, reject, suggest amendments, or keep the Bill for 6 months.

Deadlock: If disagreement persists, a Joint Sitting may be called.

5

Joint Sitting (Article 108)

Presided by the Speaker of Lok Sabha. Called when there's deadlock between both Houses.

Allowed for:

Ordinary Bills only

Not allowed for:

Money Bills, Constitutional Amendment Bills

6

President's Assent (Article 111)

The Bill goes to President for final approval.

Give Assent

Bill becomes an Act

Withhold Assent

Bill fails (rare)

Return Bill

Only for Ordinary Bills, must reconsider

Act Becomes Law

Once President signs, the Bill becomes a Law of India and is enforceable.

How a Money Bill Becomes Law

Key Differences from Ordinary Bill

Only in Lok Sabha
Speaker certifies
Rajya Sabha: 14 days delay only
Steps for Money Bill:
1. Introduced ONLY in Lok Sabha
2. Speaker certifies it as Money Bill
3. Sent to Rajya Sabha for suggestions
4. Rajya Sabha can delay for 14 days only
5. Lok Sabha may accept/reject suggestions
6. President must give assent (cannot return)
Memory Trick for Money Bill

"14 days, Speaker decides, Lok Sabha dominates"

14 days delay by Rajya Sabha
Speaker's decision is final
Lok Sabha has exclusive power
President cannot return it

Law-Making in State Legislatures

Ordinary Bill in States
Introduction: Either House (if bicameral)
Council Power: Can delay maximum 4 months (2+1+1)
Assembly Power: Final authority in legislation
Governor's Role: May give assent, withhold, return, or reserve for President
Money Bill in States
Introduction: ONLY in Legislative Assembly
Council Power: Can delay only 14 days
Speaker's Role: Certifies as Money Bill
Governor's Role: Cannot return Money Bill
Governor's Options for State Bills
Give Assent

Bill becomes law

Withhold Assent

Bill fails

Return Bill

Except Money Bill

Reserve for President

For President's consideration

SSC MTS Practice Questions

1. Who certifies a Bill as a Money Bill?

Speaker of Lok Sabha

2. Money Bill can be introduced in which House?

Lok Sabha only

3. Rajya Sabha can delay Money Bill for how many days?

14 days

4. Joint Sitting of Parliament is presided by?

Speaker of Lok Sabha

5. Joint Sitting is allowed for which type of Bills?

Ordinary Bills only

6. President can return which type of Bill?

Only Ordinary Bill (not Money Bill or Constitutional Amendment)

7. Constitutional Amendment Bill requires what type of majority?

Special majority (2/3rd of members present and voting)

8. Governor may reserve a State Bill for whose consideration?

President of India

9. Legislative Council can delay an Ordinary Bill for maximum how long?

4 months (2+1+1 months)

10. Which Article defines Money Bill?

Article 110

Conclusion

Mastering the law-making process is essential for SSC exams. This topic covers the complete journey of a bill from introduction to becoming a law, including key differences between various types of bills.

Key Takeaways

Types of Bills: Ordinary, Money, Finance, Constitutional Amendment
Money Bill: Only in Lok Sabha, Speaker certifies, Rajya Sabha 14-day delay
Ordinary Bill: Can be in either House, Joint Sitting allowed for deadlock
Joint Sitting: Article 108, Speaker presides, not for Money/Amendment Bills
President's Assent: Can return Ordinary Bills only
State Bills: Governor can reserve for President, Council delay 4 months
Memory Trick: "I S S T P J P" for stages, "14 days, Speaker decides" for Money Bill
