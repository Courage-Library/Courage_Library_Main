Citizenship (Articles 5–11)

Indian Polity - Complete Notes for SSC MTS, GD, CHSL

Introduction

Citizenship in India defines the legal relationship between an individual and the state, granting rights like voting, holding public office, and protection under the Constitution. Articles 5–11 of the Constitution and the Citizenship Act, 1955 govern this crucial aspect.

SSC frequently asks about citizenship acquisition methods, key articles, CAA provisions, and OCI/PIO status.

PART 1: Articles 5 to 11 – Citizenship at Commencement

Historical Context

These articles determined who became Indian citizens when the Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950, addressing post-partition complexities.

5
Article 5 – Citizenship by Domicile

Person became citizen if they met any condition:

Born in India
Either parent born in India
Ordinary residence for 5 years before 26 Jan 1950
6
Article 6 – Migrants from Pakistan

People who migrated from Pakistan to India:

Before 19 July 1948 → Automatic
After 19 July 1948 → Registration Certificate required
7
Article 7 – Migrants to Pakistan

People who migrated to Pakistan after 1 March 1947:

Not considered Indian citizen
Exception: Returned with resettlement permit
8
Article 8 – Indians Abroad

People of Indian origin living outside India could register as citizens at Indian consulates.

Applicable to pre-1950 diaspora
9
Article 9 – No Dual Citizenship

Voluntarily acquiring foreign citizenship results in loss of Indian citizenship.

India does NOT allow dual citizenship
10/11
Articles 10 & 11
Article 10: Continuation of citizenship
Article 11: Parliament empowered to make citizenship laws

PART 2: Citizenship Act, 1955 (5 Ways of Acquisition)

5 Ways

To acquire Indian citizenship under the Citizenship Act, 1955

B
1. By Birth
1950–1987:

Anyone born in India

1987–2004:

At least one parent Indian

After 2004:

Both parents Indian (or one Indian + other not illegal migrant)

D
2. By Descent
Born outside India:

At least one parent Indian citizen at time of birth

Registration:

Must register at Indian consulate within one year

R
3. By Registration
Person of Indian Origin (PIO) – 7 years residence
Married to Indian citizen – 7 years residence
Minor children of Indian citizens
Citizens of Commonwealth countries
N
4. By Naturalization
Residence:

11 years total (12 months immediately before + 10 years)

Requirements:

Good character, knowledge of Indian language, renunciation of previous citizenship

I
5. By Incorporation of Territory
When India acquires new territory:

People of that territory become Indian citizens by law

Examples:
Goa (1961)
Pondicherry (1962)
Sikkim (1975)
Memory Trick: "B–D–R–N–I"

Birth – Descent – Registration – Naturalization – Incorporation

Easy way to remember all 5 methods of acquiring Indian citizenship

PART 3: OCI & PIO Status

PIO – Person of Indian Origin
Status: Not a citizen of India
Benefits: Special visa facilities, property rights
Card: PIO Card (now merged with OCI)
Key Restriction: Cannot vote or hold constitutional posts
OCI – Overseas Citizen of India
Status: Not full citizenship (no dual citizenship)
Benefits: Multiple entry, multi-purpose visa, parity with NRIs
Restrictions: No voting rights, no government jobs, no constitutional posts
Eligibility: Foreign nationals of Indian origin up to 4 generations
Important Note

India does NOT allow dual citizenship. OCI is a lifelong visa status, not citizenship.

PIO Card scheme merged with OCI Card in 2015. Existing PIO Cards valid until expiry.

PART 4: Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA), 2019

Simplified Explanation for SSC

CAA provides accelerated pathway to Indian citizenship for persecuted religious minorities from three neighboring countries.

Applicable Countries
Pakistan
Afghanistan
Bangladesh
Eligible Communities (6 Religions)
Hindu
Sikh
Buddhist
Jain
Parsi
Christian
Key Provisions
Fast-track citizenship: 6 years instead of 11 years
Cut-off date: Entered India on or before 31 December 2014
Exemption: From prosecution under Foreigners Act, 1946
Important Clarifications
Does NOT take away anyone's existing citizenship
Does NOT apply to Indian citizens (only to migrants)
Muslims not included as they are majority in those countries
Focus on religious minorities facing persecution

SSC MTS Practice Questions

1. Which Article deals with citizenship by domicile at the commencement of the Constitution?

Article 5

2. Which Article states that acquiring foreign citizenship results in loss of Indian citizenship?

Article 9

3. Which Article empowers Parliament to make laws regarding citizenship?

Article 11

4. In which year was the Citizenship Act passed?

1955

5. Does India allow dual citizenship?

No, India does not allow dual citizenship

6. How many ways are there to acquire Indian citizenship under the Citizenship Act?

5 ways

7. How many years of residence are required for citizenship by naturalization?

11 years (12 months immediately before + 10 years)

8. Under Article 6, who got Indian citizenship?

Migrants from Pakistan to India

9. What does OCI stand for?

Overseas Citizen of India

10. CAA 2019 applies to which three countries?

Pakistan, Afghanistan, and Bangladesh

Conclusion

Understanding Indian citizenship is fundamental to grasping constitutional rights and obligations. The provisions in Articles 5–11 and the Citizenship Act, 1955, along with recent amendments like CAA, form a comprehensive framework.

Key Takeaways

Articles 5–11: Deal with citizenship at Constitution's commencement
No Dual Citizenship: Article 9 prohibits dual citizenship
5 Acquisition Methods: Birth, Descent, Registration, Naturalization, Incorporation
Citizenship Act: 1955 – Main legislation governing citizenship
OCI: Overseas status, not citizenship, with limited rights
CAA 2019: Fast-track citizenship for persecuted minorities from 3 countries
Memory Tricks: "B M R O F C P" for Articles, "B–D–R–N–I" for acquisition methods
