Revolt of 1857 – India's First War of Independence

Complete Notes for SSC MTS, GD, CHSL - Modern History

Introduction

The Revolt of 1857 was the first large-scale rebellion against British rule in India. It spread across North India and significantly challenged British authority, marking a turning point in India's freedom struggle.

This is one of the MOST IMPORTANT topics for SSC exams. Expect 3–4 questions from this chapter in MTS/GD/CHSL.

The Great Revolt

Causes of the Revolt of 1857

The revolt resulted from multiple political, economic, social, religious, and military factors.

1. Political Causes

Doctrine of Lapse

States annexed by Lord Dalhousie:

Satara
Jhansi
Nagpur
Sambalpur

Annexation of Awadh (1856)

British annexed Awadh claiming "mis-governance", angering many soldiers who were from Awadh.

Removal of Indian Kings

Systematic takeover of kingdoms in Rajasthan, Punjab, and other regions created resentment.

2. Economic Causes

Heavy Taxes

Farmers forced to pay high land revenue leading to poverty and famines.

Destruction of Industries

British machine-made goods ruined Indian handloom and handicraft industries.

Drain of Wealth

Systematic transfer of wealth from India to England.

3. Social & Religious Causes

Indians feared:

  • Forced conversion to Christianity
  • Christian missionaries interfering in traditions
  • Social reforms seen as cultural interference
  • Rumors of British destroying Indian culture

Laws like Widow Remarriage Act (1856) were misunderstood as interference in religious practices.

4. Military Causes

Poor treatment of Indian soldiers in British army:

  • No promotion opportunities
  • Low salaries compared to British soldiers
  • Humiliating behavior by British officers

Key Fact

The Bengal Army had 85% Indian soldiers, making military discontent a major factor.

5. Immediate Cause (Trigger Point)

Enfield Rifle Cartridges

  • Cartridges greased with cow and pig fat
  • Cow = holy for Hindus
  • Pig = forbidden for Muslims
  • Soldiers had to bite them to load rifles

Mangal Pandey

On 29 March 1857, at Barrackpore, Mangal Pandey refused to use the cartridges and attacked British officers, becoming the first hero of the revolt.

Spread of the Revolt (Important Centers & Leaders)

1. Meerut – Where It Began (10 May 1857)

Soldiers rose in rebellion, killed British officers, and marched to Delhi, marking the beginning of widespread revolt.

2. Delhi – Symbolic Center

Leader: Bahadur Shah Zafar (last Mughal emperor)

He became the symbolic head of the revolt, though he was old and reluctant.

Delhi was recaptured by British in September 1857 after heavy fighting.

3. Kanpur

Leader: Nana Saheb

Key associates: Tantia Tope and Azimullah Khan

Nana Saheb declared himself Peshwa and led fierce resistance against British forces.

4. Lucknow

Leader: Begum Hazrat Mahal

She fought to restore her son Birjis Qadr to the throne of Awadh and organized strong resistance.

5. Jhansi (Most Famous)

Leader: Rani Lakshmibai (Jhansi ki Rani)

Fought with exceptional bravery and military skill.

Died in June 1858 during the Battle of Gwalior, becoming a legendary freedom fighter.

6. Bareilly

Leader: Khan Bahadur Khan

7. Bihar

Leader: Kunwar Singh

80-year-old zamindar who fought till the end

8. Faizabad

Leader: Maulavi Ahmadullah

Why the Revolt Failed? (Major Reasons)

1. Lack of Unity & Limited Spread

Only North India participated actively. South India, Bengal, and Punjab largely remained quiet or supported British.

2. No Modern Weapons

British had superior guns, cannons, and military technology while rebels fought with traditional weapons.

3. No Common Leadership

Local leaders fought separately without coordinated strategy. Bahadur Shah Zafar was a symbolic leader without real power.

4. Lack of Clear Planning

The revolt was spontaneous rather than well-planned. No unified strategy or timing.

5. No National Feeling

People fought for local kings and personal grievances rather than for India as a nation. The concept of Indian nationalism was not yet developed.

End of Revolt

By 1858, the British had completely suppressed the revolt through military force and strategic recapture of key centers.

Results of the Revolt (MOST IMPORTANT)

1. End of East India Company Rule

The British Government took direct control of India through the Government of India Act 1858.

2. Queen's Proclamation (1858)

  • Promised not to annex Indian states
  • Assured religious freedom
  • Promised equal treatment to Indians

3. Beginning of British Raj

India became a direct colony ruled by the British Crown. The Viceroy replaced the Governor-General.

4. Reorganization of Army

  • More British soldiers recruited
  • Fewer Indian soldiers in key positions
  • Artillery kept exclusively with British

Long-term Significance

Though the revolt failed, it laid the foundation for India's organized freedom movement. It inspired future generations to fight for independence.

Conclusion

The Revolt of 1857 was the first major organized attempt to overthrow British rule in India. Though it failed militarily, it became a symbol of resistance and laid the foundation for India's organized freedom movement.
