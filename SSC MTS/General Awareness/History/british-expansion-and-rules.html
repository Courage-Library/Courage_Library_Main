British Expansion in India & Their Policies

Complete Notes for SSC MTS, GD, CHSL, Railways - Modern History

Introduction

After the Battle of Plassey (1757) and Battle of Buxar (1764), the British East India Company rapidly expanded across India using wars, treaties, and strategic policies.

This is one of the most important chapters for SSC exams. Questions frequently come from annexation policies, governors-general, and major wars.

1. Important Battles for British Expansion

Battle of Plassey (1757)

  • British (Robert Clive) vs. Siraj-ud-Daulah
  • British victory due to Mir Jafar's betrayal
  • Marked the beginning of British political power in India

Significance

First major British victory → Foundation of British rule in India

Battle of Buxar (1764)

British vs. Combined forces of:

Mir Qasim
Shuja-ud-Daula
Shah Alam II

Outcome & Significance

  • British got Diwani rights of Bengal, Bihar, Odisha
  • Became real rulers of India
  • Mughal emperor became British pensioner

Anglo-Mysore Wars (1767–1799)

  • Four wars against Hyder Ali & Tipu Sultan
  • Tipu Sultan died in 4th war (1799) at Srirangapatna
  • Mysore came under British control

Memory Trick: "Mysore = 4 Wars (Tipu died in last one)"

Anglo-Maratha Wars (1775–1818)

  • Three wars between British and Marathas
  • Final outcome: Marathas defeated
  • British gained control over most of India

Memory Trick: "M-M-S" → Mysore, Marathas, Sikhs (Major enemies)

2. British Annexation Policies (VERY IMPORTANT)

A. Subsidiary Alliance

Introduced by Lord Wellesley (1798)

Features:

  • Indian rulers must accept British troops in their state
  • Must pay for their maintenance
  • No foreign relations without British permission
  • If failed to pay → British would take over

States Annexed:

Hyderabad
Awadh
Mysore
Tanjavur

B. Doctrine of Lapse

Introduced by Lord Dalhousie (1848)

Rule:

If an Indian ruler had no natural male heir, his kingdom would be annexed by the British.

States Annexed:

Satara
Jhansi
Nagpur
Sambalpur

Memory Trick: "J–N–S–S" → Jhansi, Nagpur, Satara, Sambalpur

C. Policy of Paramountcy

British claimed themselves as supreme power in India. All Indian rulers had to follow their orders.

This policy was used to justify interference in princely states' internal matters.

3. Major British Governors-General & Their Policies

Governor-General Period Key Policies/Achievements
Warren Hastings 1773–1785 First Governor-General of Bengal, Regulating Act (1773), Supreme Court in Calcutta
Lord Cornwallis 1786–1793 Civil Services reforms, Permanent Settlement in Bengal (1793)
Lord Wellesley 1798–1805 Subsidiary Alliance, Expansionist policies
Lord Bentinck 1828–1835 Abolished Sati (1829), abolished Thuggee, promoted English education
Lord Dalhousie 1848–1856 Doctrine of Lapse, Railways (1853), Telegraph, Postal system
Lord Canning 1856–1862 First Viceroy, Sepoy Mutiny (1857), Widow Remarriage Act (1856)

Memory Trick for Governors-General

"H–C–W–B–D–C"

Hastings → Cornwallis → Wellesley → Bentinck → Dalhousie → Canning

4. Economic Policies of the British

These policies made India poor and Britain rich through systematic exploitation.

A. Land Revenue Systems

1. Permanent Settlement (1793)

  • Introduced by Lord Cornwallis
  • Zamindari system in Bengal
  • Fixed revenue permanently

2. Ryotwari System

  • Introduced by Thomas Munro
  • Direct settlement with farmers (Ryots)
  • Mainly in South India

3. Mahalwari System

  • Introduced by Holt Mackenzie
  • Settlement with village community
  • North-West Provinces

Memory Trick: "Z–R–M" = Bengal (Zamindari) – South (Ryotwari) – North (Mahalwari)

B. Deindustrialization

British systematically destroyed Indian handloom and handicraft industries to promote British manufactured goods.

C. Drain of Wealth

Explained by Dadabhai Naoroji in his book "Poverty and Un-British Rule in India".

Wealth was systematically transferred from India to Britain.

D. Commercialization of Agriculture

Farmers were forced to grow cash crops instead of food crops, leading to famines:

Indigo
Cotton
Tea
Opium

5. Social & Cultural Policies

Education Policies

  • Macaulay's Minutes (1835) → English education promoted
  • Wood's Despatch (1854) → Modern education framework
  • Establishment of universities in Calcutta, Bombay, Madras (1857)

Social Reforms

  • Abolition of Sati (1829) - Lord Bentinck
  • Widow Remarriage Act (1856) - Lord Canning
  • Ban on female infanticide
  • Abolition of Thuggee

Press Laws

British implemented various press laws to restrict newspaper freedom and control public opinion.

6. Impact of British Rule

Positive Impacts

  • Railways and transportation networks
  • Telegraph and communication systems
  • Modern postal system
  • English education and modern universities
  • Modern legal system

Negative Impacts

  • Famines and widespread poverty
  • Unemployment and deindustrialization
  • Destruction of traditional industries
  • Heavy taxation and economic exploitation
  • Loss of sovereignty and political freedom

SSC MTS Practice Questions

1. Who introduced the Doctrine of Lapse?

Lord Dalhousie (1848)

2. Subsidiary Alliance was introduced by which Governor-General?

Lord Wellesley (1798)

3. Permanent Settlement was introduced in which year?

1793 (by Lord Cornwallis)

4. In which battle did Tipu Sultan die?

4th Anglo-Mysore War (1799)

5. Who explained the "Drain of Wealth" theory?

Dadabhai Naoroji

6. Who abolished the practice of Sati?

Lord William Bentinck (1829)

7. Which battle gave British Diwani rights of Bengal?

Battle of Buxar (1764)

Conclusion

British expansion was a systematic, step-by-step takeover using battles, treaties, annexation policies, and economic pressure. Understanding these policies and their impact is crucial for scoring 4–6 marks easily in SSC MTS, GD, and CHSL exams.
